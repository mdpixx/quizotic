## SECTION 1: Indie Hackers Build Story

# I built a live quiz platform while working 9-to-5 at IndianOil

I am 41, I have a family, and I work full-time as a Senior Manager in Learning & Development at IndianOil.

That is not the typical opening line for a SaaS launch post. Most founder stories seem to begin with someone quitting their job, moving fast, and raising money. Mine began after office hours, usually after dinner, when the house became quiet and I could steal two focused hours to build.

The product is called Quizotic. It is a live quiz and interactive presentation platform at quizotic.live.

The simple version is: Kahoot-style quizzes plus Mentimeter-style interaction, built with Indian classrooms and training rooms in mind. The deeper version is: a tool that respects how people actually learn.

The problem came from my day job. In corporate learning, we run workshops, onboarding modules, compliance programs, and safety briefings. In schools and coaching institutes, teachers fight the same battle from a different angle. Everyone has the same question: how do I know if people are learning, not just sitting through the session?

Most sessions still end with a polite "Any questions?" and silence.

That silence is dangerous. It can mean people understood. It can also mean they are tired, shy, confused, or afraid of looking foolish. In Indian classrooms, a student may not raise a hand even when the concept is unclear. In corporate training, an employee may nod through a safety topic and still walk away with the wrong mental model.

Quizzes solve part of this. But many quiz tools are built mainly for energy: fast answers, leaderboards, music, cheering. That is useful, but it is not enough. A trainer also needs to know what kind of thinking the question tested. Was it recall? Application? Analysis? Did the class understand, or did they guess well?

That is where Quizotic started for me.

I wanted live engagement, but I also wanted learning science built in from the beginning: Bloom's Taxonomy tagging, confidence tracking, and spaced retrieval. Bloom's shows whether a quiz is stuck at memory level or pushing learners toward application and analysis. Confidence tracking exposes the most dangerous quadrant: people who are confidently wrong. Spaced retrieval brings missed concepts back after the session, because one-shot learning does not survive real life.

The first version was not glamorous. I built it at night with AI as a coding partner. The stack became Next.js, Socket.io, PostgreSQL, Prisma, Razorpay, and Railway. Socket.io mattered because when the host moves to the next question, every participant phone needs to move with it.

The earliest build did the basic thing: create a quiz, start a room, show a six-digit code, collect answers, show a leaderboard. It was enough to feel the product and reveal the rough edges.

Participants do not want an app install. Hosts do not want a 20-step setup. A trainer standing in front of a room cannot debug software while 40 people wait. The join page has to be light. The room code has to be obvious. The host journey has to flow.

That last word became important. I recently changed the quiz creation journey because the earlier version felt too much like software. A teacher or trainer is not trying to "configure an object." They are trying to prepare a learning moment.

The first feedback was practical, not poetic.

People asked: Can participants join without logging in? Can I use it on mobile? Can I create from a PDF? Can I run it with 40 or 50 people? Can I use it for a corporate workshop, not only a classroom? Can the report tell me who struggled? Can it work on weak Wi-Fi?

Quizotic now supports live quizzes, interactive presentations, polls, word clouds, open-ended responses, rankings, ratings, image questions, live leaderboards, real-time analytics, AI quiz generation, and learning-science reports. The free plan is designed to be genuinely usable: up to 50 participants per session, no participant account required, and a browser-based experience.

I am also being honest about where it is today.

Current MRR is ₹0. Quizotic is pre-revenue. I have built pricing and payments around INR, UPI, and Razorpay, but this is not yet a revenue story. The goal is to get real usage from teachers, trainers, coaching institutes, and event hosts, then earn the right to charge.

- Quizzes created: [insert verified number]
- Live sessions run: [insert verified number]

- MRR: ₹0

The emotional part of building this has been balancing identities.

At 9-to-5, I am an L&D professional with responsibilities, deadlines, and people depending on me. At home, I am a husband and father. At night, I become a builder trying to turn years of training-room frustration into software. Some nights I make visible progress. Some nights I spend two hours fixing one redirect or one broken mobile layout.

There is no romantic version of this. It is slow, tiring, and deeply satisfying.

The advantage of building from inside the problem is that I do not have to imagine the user. I have stood in the room and seen the difference between "people attended" and "people understood."

That is what I want Quizotic to create more often.

The short-term goal is simple: get 50 serious beta users who run real sessions and tell me what broke, what helped, and what they would pay for. The next goal is to make Quizotic the easiest live quiz and interactive presentation tool for Indian educators and trainers: fast to start, light on bandwidth, priced in INR, and useful beyond the leaderboard.

Longer term, I want Quizotic to become a learning intelligence layer for classrooms and training rooms. Not just "who won the quiz?" but "what did this group understand, where are they overconfident, what should we revisit, and when?"

If you are a teacher, corporate trainer, coaching institute owner, or anyone who runs learning sessions, I would love your feedback.

The product is live at quizotic.live.

It is early. It is imperfect. But it is built from a real problem I have lived with for years.
